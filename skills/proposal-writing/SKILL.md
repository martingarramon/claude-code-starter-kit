# Skill: Proposal Writing

## When to Use
Activate when drafting a client proposal, statement of work, project pitch, or any
document that asks someone to hire you or approve a project.

## Core Principle
The client does not care about your process. They care about their problem getting solved.
Lead with their pain, not your resume.

## Structure

### 1. Problem
State the client's problem in their language, not yours. Show that you understand what
they are dealing with and what it is costing them (time, money, missed opportunities).
Two to three sentences. No generic filler.

### 2. Approach
Describe how you will solve it. Be specific enough to show competence, brief enough to
stay readable. Mention key technologies or methods only if they matter to the outcome.
Avoid jargon the client hasn't used themselves.

### 3. Deliverables
List exactly what the client will receive. Each deliverable should be concrete and
verifiable -- something the client can look at and say "yes, I got this" or "no, I didn't."

### 4. Timeline
Break the work into phases with durations. Tie phases to deliverables. The client should
be able to map any deliverable to a date range.

### 5. Investment
State the price clearly. If there are options (tiers, phases, hourly vs fixed), present
them as simple choices, not a pricing matrix. Always tie cost back to value.

### 6. Next Step
End with one clear action. Not "let me know your thoughts" -- something specific:
"Book a 30-minute call to walk through the approach" or "Reply to confirm and I will
send the contract by Friday."

## Voice
- Confident, not arrogant. You know how to do this. You don't need to prove it with
  buzzwords.
- Specific, not generic. Replace "we have extensive experience" with a concrete example.
- Brief. Every sentence should earn its place. If it doesn't advance the client's
  understanding or confidence, cut it.
- No filler phrases: "leverage," "synergize," "cutting-edge," "best-in-class."

## Rules
- Read the client's original request carefully. Mirror their terminology.
- Never fabricate case studies, metrics, or references.
- If you don't know the client's budget range, present 2-3 tiers.
- Always proofread for specificity -- vague proposals lose to specific ones.
